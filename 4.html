Your handwriting is chicken scratch, Handwriting that is difficult to read. Origin: It’s said that the initially started as writing to looking like chicken feet. The natural evolution from feet to scratch seems to make sense as a visual depiction of the act of chicken’s scratching and the result it leaves on the ground. Several references to origin are reported by the online resource poetry-contingency: A German work as translated in English by Sofie Delffs, Leipzig, 1872, depicts the saying by description, “Hornebog, drawing his sword, pierced the parchment with it, and presented it to his companion, steels on the point of the blade. What do you these hooks and chicken feet mean?” In 1899, the publication Gentleman Farmer, vol. 6 stated, “The hand-writing in our public schools looks now like chicken feet, and should our children be compelled to study the chicken language they would soon become chicken- hearted too.” T.W. Higginson described Emily Dickonson’s writing in 1891 as “famous fossil bird tracks.” In 1909, a character in the play “Roger of Rutland: A Drama in Four Acts” by Lewis F. Bostelmann describes his character’s writing (who happens to be Shakespeare) as “…that resembles more a chicken’s feet that puts the art of writing to shame.” Source: https://poetry-contingency.uwaterloo.ca/chicken- scratch/ AI: Midjourney -- chicken_feet_chicken_writing_signature
